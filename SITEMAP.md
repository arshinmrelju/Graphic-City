# GraphicCity — Complete Sitemap & User Journey

---

## Navigation Structure

### Primary Navigation (Header)

```
[Logo]   Work   Capabilities   About   Journal   [Start a Project]
```

- **Logo** — Always linked to Home. Doubles as a top-left anchor.
- **Work** — First nav item. Proof is the strongest persuader. Leads to the case study grid.
- **Capabilities** — Second. Prospects who want to evaluate fit need to know what we do before they invest time in our story.
- **About** — Third. After they see what we make and what we offer, they want to know who we are.
- **Journal** — Fourth. For returning visitors and content-driven discovery. Lower urgency.
- **Start a Project** — Primary CTA button. High-contrast visual anchor at the right edge. Always visible, always clickable. This is the money button.

### Secondary Navigation (Utility)

Minimal. No header clutter.
- Appears in footer only:
  - Privacy Policy
  - Terms of Service
- No "Careers" or "Press" in primary nav — those live in footer as tertiary paths.

## Page-by-Page Sitemap

---

### 1. Home

**Goal:** Make an immediate, undeniable impression of quality. Establish credibility in under 5 seconds. Drive visitors to Work (proof) or Start a Project (conversion).

**Sections (top to bottom):**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | Full-viewport. Large display type: a single, powerful statement. Subtle motion (background element, text reveal). No carousel. | You get one chance. A single, confident message lands harder than a rotating slideshow. The motion signals craft without demanding attention. |
| **Featured Work** | 2–3 full-bleed project cards. Large mockup or video thumbnail. Project name + discipline + "View Case Study" link. | Show, don't tell. The fastest way to build belief is to put the work in front of them immediately. Limited to 3 so the page stays tight. |
| **Process Teaser** | 3-step horizontal strip. Label + 1-line description per step. No detail — just the shape of how we think. | Visitors need to know we have a method. They don't need the full method yet. Enough to differentiate from "we'll figure it out" agencies. |
| **Selected Clients** | Logos in a single row. Grayscale or duotone. Unlinked. No tier labels. | Social proof without boasting. The logos themselves do the work. Grayscale keeps visual noise low. |
| **Testimonial** | One powerful, pull-quote style testimonial. Large type. Attribution + title. | A single, specific voice is more believable than a carousel of generic praise. Rotate on refresh. |
| **CTA Section** | "Let's make something lasting." Paragraph: one sentence on philosophy. One button: "Start a Project." No secondary link. | Clean exit from the page. One action. No decision fatigue. |

### 2. Work (Case Studies Grid)

**Goal:** Prove capability and build desire. Allow prospects to self-identify with relevant projects.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Page Header** | "Selected Work." Subtext: "Each project is a partnership. These are some of the stories we're proudest of." | Sets the tone — curated, not a portfolio dump. "Selected" implies high standards. |
| **Filter Bar** | Optional: All / Brand Identity / Digital Product / Art Direction / Motion | Low friction filter. Helps prospects find relevance fast. Default: All. |
| **Project Grid** | 3-column grid (desktop). Each card: full-bleed thumbnail, project name, discipline, year. Click → Case Study page. | The grid itself is the proof. Cards are clean — no buttons, no text blocks. The work is the thumbnail. |
| **End-of-page CTA** | "See something you like?" → Start a Project | Warm transition from proof to action. |

---

### 3. Case Study (Individual)

**Goal:** Immerse the prospect in the depth of our thinking. Build trust through transparency.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | Full-bleed project hero image or video. Project name + client + discipline overlay. | Sets the visual stage. The work deserves a full-viewport entrance. |
| **Credits** | Client name, our role, year. Small, elegant. | Transparency. No mystery about who did what. |
| **Context** | 2–3 paragraphs: Who the client is, what they needed, why it mattered. | The reader needs to understand the problem before they can appreciate the solution. |
| **The Approach** | Our thinking. Process sketches or strategy artifacts optional. | Demonstrates rigor. Shows we didn't jump to visuals — we thought first. |
| **The Solution** | 3–5 high-fidelity visuals. Full-width. Minimal text. Let the work speak. | The payoff. Every visual is a selling point. Text stays out of the way. |
| **Results** | Quantitative if available (e.g., "33% increase in conversion"). Qualitative if not ("The brand was described as 'instantly iconic'"). | Proof of impact. This section is optional — not every project has hard metrics, and fabricating them erodes trust. |
| **Client Testimonial** | Specific quote from the client about working with us. | Third-party validation. More powerful than anything we can say about ourselves. |
| **Next / Previous** | Links to adjacent case studies. | Keeps the prospect in the Work flow. Encourages browsing multiple projects. |
| **CTA** | "Want to see what we could do together?" → Start a Project | Conversion exit. |

### 4. Capabilities

**Goal:** Define scope without sounding like a service menu. Make the visitor feel we understand their needs at every stage.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Page Header** | "What we do." Subtext: "We partner with organizations at every stage — from founding moments to brand evolutions." | Sets breadth without sounding generic. |
| **Capability Clusters** | 4–5 clusters. Each: icon + label + 2-line description. Clusters: **Brand Strategy** (positioning, naming, messaging architecture), **Visual Identity** (logo, color, typography, art direction), **Digital Design** (web, product UI, design systems), **Motion & Interactive** (animation, micro-interactions, video), **Brand Guidance** (guidelines, templates, launch assets). | Clusters prevent a laundry-list feel. Each is a meaningful offering, not a line item. Language is project-oriented, not feature-oriented. |
| **Process Deep-Dive** | 4-phase timeline: **Discover** (research, stakeholder interviews, audit), **Define** (strategy, positioning, direction), **Design** (exploration, refinement, delivery), **Deploy** (guidelines, assets, launch support). Each phase: label + what to expect + optional expand for detail. | Prospects buy process as much as output. Showing a structured method signals reliability. Expandable detail keeps the page scannable. |
| **Collaboration Model** | How we work: dedicated lead designer, direct communication, no account managers, fixed-scope phases with flexible output. | Differentiator. Most agencies route through account managers. We don't. This is a selling point that warrants its own section. |
| **CTA** | "Sound like your kind of partner?" → Start a Project | Transitions from education to action. |

### 5. About

**Goal:** Humanize the studio. Build emotional connection. Reinforce the values from the brand foundation.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | Studio photo or cinematic environmental shot. Overlay text: "We are GraphicCity." | A face to the name. Studios are people, not faceless entities. |
| **Manifesto** | Short-form brand story (3–4 sentences). The "why." | The emotional core. Why we exist beyond "making things look good." |
| **Values** | The 6 core values (from brand foundation). Each: label + 1-sentence explanation. | Values are decision-making frameworks. Prospects who share them will trust us more. |
| **Team** | Not individual headshots. Instead: a single candid team photo (editorial style) + "Meet the team" with a brief collective philosophy. Or individual, but keep it tight — title + one authentic sentence. | Avoids ego-driven "our team" pages. The collective matters more than individual accolades. If individual, ensure every entry feels human, not resume-like. |
| **Process** | Visual timeline of a real past project from start to finish (high-level). | Makes the abstract concrete. Shows exactly what a partnership looks like in practice. |
| **CTA** | "Ready to start a conversation?" → Start a Project | Warm invitation. |

### 6. Journal

**Goal:** Demonstrate expertise, attract organic discovery, and nurture prospects who aren't ready to buy.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Page Header** | "Journal." Subtext: "Thoughts on craft, process, and building brands that last." | Editorial tone. Not a blog. A journal implies curation and care. |
| **Featured Article** | Largest card. Hero image. Title. Pullquote. "Read" link. | Best content gets prime placement. Signals quality. |
| **Article Grid** | 2-column grid. Card: thumbnail, category tag, title, author, date, read time. | Scannable. Category tags help with filtering. No excerpts — titles should be descriptive enough. |
| **Categories** | Optional sidebar or top filter: All / Process / Brand Strategy / Design / Culture | Helps returning readers find relevant content. |
| **End-of-page** | "Get the newsletter" — email signup. 1 field. No spam promise. | Nurture. Builds the retargeting list. Low friction — one field only. |

Each article page follows a clean editorial template with generous reading width, pull quotes, full-bleed media, and a related articles footer.

### 7. Start a Project (Inquiry / Contact)

**Goal:** Convert. Make the inquiry process feel as premium as the output. Set expectations immediately.

This is the most UX-critical page on the site. It is the conversion moment.

**Sections:**

| Section | Content | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Tiered CTAs** | Three paths, visually equal: **1. Start a Project** (new brand, redesign, campaign) — leads to brief form. **2. Partnership Inquiry** (ongoing retainer, fractional creative direction) — leads to a simpler form. **3. General Inquiry** (speaking, press, collaboration) — leads to email link. | One-size-fits-all forms feel cold. Tiering signals we understand different needs. Also qualifies intent on our side. |
| **The Brief Form** | 8 fields max: Name / Company / Email / Project Type (dropdown) / Budget Range (select: Under 25k / 25–75k / 75–150k / 150k+) / Timeline / Project Description (long text) / How did you hear about us? | Long enough to qualify, short enough to not abandon. Budget question is essential for fit — but we offer ranges, not a hard number. Optional "How did you hear" for attribution. |
| **What Happens Next** | 3-step micro-journey: 1. We review within 48 hours. 2. We schedule a call if there's alignment. 3. We send a tailored proposal. | Reduces anxiety. The prospect knows exactly what to expect after hitting submit. Certainty = trust. |
| **Social Proof** | 1–2 testimonials or a client logo row. | Reinforces that submitting is a safe, smart decision. |
| **FAQ** | Expandable accordion. 6–8 questions: "What does a project cost?" / "How long does a brand take?" / "Do you work with early-stage startups?" / "What if we're not ready to commit?" / "Do you offer rush timelines?" / "What do you need from us?" / "Can we see examples in our industry?" | Answers objections before they stop a submission. Each question is a real objection we've heard. |

## User Journey Maps

### Journey A — The Direct Converter (Highest Intent)

```
Land → Home → [Start a Project] → Brief Form → Submit
```
Visitor already knows what they want. Home validates their confidence in 3 seconds, then they go straight to inquiry. No friction. No unnecessary steps.

### Journey B — The Diligent Buyer (Majority of Traffic)

```
Land → Home → Work → Case Study (×2–3) → About → [Start a Project] → Brief Form → Submit
```
Needs proof before commitment. Moves from proof → trust → action. Average 4–7 page views. This is the most common path. Each page must naturally flow to the next.

### Journey C — The Researcher (Early Stage)

```
Land → Home → Journal → Journal Article (×2–3) → About → Work → [Start a Project] → Brief Form → Submit
```
Not ready to buy. Building trust through content. May take multiple sessions. Retargeting via newsletter or remarketing pixels is essential. Journal becomes the lead nurturing engine.

### Journey D — The Referral (High Trust)

```
Land → [Start a Project] → Brief Form → Submit
```
Arrives via referral or direct link. Needs minimal convincing. The site's job is to confirm their referral's recommendation, not to sell from scratch. Home page is the confirmation moment.

### Journey E — The Services Evaluator

```
Land → Capabilities → Work → About → [Start a Project]
```
Needs to verify scope fit first. Capabilities page is their entry point. They need to confirm we do what they need before they invest in our proof.

## Conversion Architecture

### Primary Conversion Event
**"Start a Project" form submission.**

### Secondary Conversion Events
- Newsletter signup (Journal)
- Case study view (warm lead signal)
- Time on site > 3 minutes (engagement signal)
- Return visit within 7 days (consideration signal)

### Friction Points (and responses)

| Friction | Solution |
|---|---|
| "I don't know if we can afford this." | Budget range field in form + FAQ on investment. Honesty upfront prevents wasted time on both sides. |
| "I'm not ready to commit." | Newsletter signup + Journal content. No hard sell. |
| "I don't know exactly what I need." | Capabilities page + process section. Help them articulate their need by showing what's possible. |
| "I've been burned by agencies before." | Case study depth + collaboration model section + client testimonials. Over-transparency builds trust. |
| "The form is too long / asks too much." | 8 fields max. Clear label on what happens after submit. Progress indicator if multi-step. |

### Post-Submission Journey

1. **Auto-response email** — Within 1 minute. Confirms receipt. Sets expectations: "We review every inquiry personally. You'll hear from us within 48 hours."
2. **Human review** — Within 48 hours. Founder or lead designer reads the brief personally.
3. **Discovery call** — If mutual fit. 30 minutes. No pitch — just conversation and questions.
4. **Proposal** — Within 5 business days of call. Tailored scope, timeline, investment.
5. **Onboarding** — Upon agreement. Welcome packet, stakeholder questionnaire, kickoff scheduling.

Every step of the post-submission journey is designed to feel personal, not automated. No sequences. No drips. Just humans responding to humans — which is itself the most premium thing a studio can offer.
